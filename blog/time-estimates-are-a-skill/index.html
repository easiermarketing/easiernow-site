Time estimates are a skill, not a personality trait

People who are bad at guessing how long work takes usually think they were born that way. It is a skill with a feedback loop, and most people have never closed the loop.

Anthony Stratton · 2 July 2026 · 4 min read

Ask someone how long a piece of work will take and you get a number. Ask them afterwards how long it took and you often get a shrug. The estimate was made with some care. The actual was never recorded. So the estimate and the outcome never met, and nothing was learned.

Do that for ten years and you end up believing you are just bad at estimating. It feels like a fixed trait, in the same category as being bad at names. It is not. It is a skill that has been practised without feedback, which is the one condition under which skills do not improve.

Why practice alone does not fix it

There is a well known finding in psychology about how expertise forms. Practice only builds skill when the practice comes with fast, clear feedback about whether you were right.

Weather forecasters are good at estimating probability, because every forecast is checked against what happened the next day. Radiologists reading certain scans are also good, for the same reason. In fields where the answer arrives years later, or never, experienced people are often no better than beginners and are considerably more confident.

Time estimation at work sits in the second group by default. You guess, you do the work, you move on. Nobody writes down what actually happened. There is no correction, so there is no learning, and confidence keeps growing anyway.

What the errors actually look like

When people do start recording, the mistakes turn out to be patterned rather than random. A few show up again and again.

You estimate the work, not the task. Writing the report is ninety minutes. The task also includes finding last quarter’s version, chasing the two numbers you are missing, the interruption halfway through, and rereading it before you send it. The work was ninety minutes. The task was three hours.

You estimate the good version of yourself. The estimate assumes a clear morning, no meetings either side, and full attention. You have had few of those days this year.

You forget the restart. A task split across two sittings is not the same length as one done in a single run. You pay to get back in, every time you come back. On heavily interrupted work this is often the largest single cost, and it is almost never in the estimate.

Long tasks are underestimated more than short ones. Something you guess at twenty minutes is usually close. Something you guess at two days can easily be five. The error grows with the size of the thing, so the biggest commitments are the ones you are worst at.

Closing the loop

The fix is unglamorous. Write the estimate down before you start. Write the actual down when you finish. Look at the pair.

You do not need a system for this. A note with two numbers on it works. What matters is that both numbers exist and that you see them next to each other, because the comparison is the entire mechanism.

Two or three weeks of this is enough to see something useful. Most people find their ratio is fairly stable. They are not wrong by a random amount. They are consistently wrong by roughly the same multiple, and often by a different multiple for different kinds of work. Admin might come in close. Anything creative might reliably take double.

Once you know your multiple, you have a correction. You are still guessing, but now you are guessing and then adjusting by a number that has evidence behind it. That is a large improvement over guessing.

Bad estimates become bad promises

Bad estimates do more than make you late. They shape the promises you make.

If you think the proposal takes two hours, you agree to send it tomorrow. It takes five, so it eats the evening, and the thing you had planned for the evening moves to the weekend. The original error was small. What it cost was your week.

Multiply that across every commitment and you get the familiar state of being constantly behind while working constantly. The plan was built on numbers that were never checked.

There is also a version of this that helps other people. When you can say “that kind of job usually takes me a day and a half, so I will have it Thursday”, you are giving a colleague or a client something they can plan around. Vague speed is worth less to them than an accurate number, even a slower one.

Start with what you finish

If you want to begin, do not start by estimating everything. Start at the other end. For the next two weeks, write down how long things actually took, whether or not you estimated them first.

Most people find the actuals more surprising than the estimates. Tasks you dreaded turn out to be short. Small jobs you never think about turn out to eat whole afternoons. That alone changes what you agree to.

Then add the estimate back in, and let the two numbers argue with each other for a month. The gap will close, because you have finally given the skill the one thing it needed, which is feedback.
